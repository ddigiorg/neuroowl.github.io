Defining Intelligence

"The beginning of wisdom is the definition of terms" -Socrates.

Understanding intelligence is difficult because it involves an ongoing, ever-evolving philosophical discussion and scientific research. Often ideas on the topic mean different things to different people. Definitions sometimes overlap with and involve other definitions, producing a confusing mess of terms. This is especially prevalent in discussions of consciousness, an extremely complex and heavily debated topic. Furthermore, the most plausible architecture of intelligence, the nervous system, is not yet well understood by the scientific community. Therefore, extracting the salient principles of intelligence from biology and engineering seems beyond anyone's ability as of now. Despite the lack of consensus on the subject, I attept to define precise, distinct, and basic relavent terms to facilitate the discussions below.

  • Environment - where entities exist and have causal relation to each other
  • Stimulus(-ae) - detectable change(s) in the environment
  • Pattern - responce to concurrent stimulae
  • Sequence - response to temporal stimulae
  • Knowledge - patterns and sequences
  • Learning- storing and/or modifying knowledge
  • Memories - stored knowledge
  • Thinking - processing memories
  • Intelligence - the ability to aquire and apply knowledge
  • Sentience - the ability to experience sensations
  • Sapience - the ability to think
  • Conscious - the ability to maintain an awareness of self and environment

"Seeing is believing"

Sensation is simply the detection of stimulation. It consists of a device called a sensor that responds to stimulus (e.g. light, sound, heat, neuron action potentials, etc). Both stimulus (input) and response (output) may be binary or analog depending on the type of sensor. Sensors have two properties:

  • Threshold - the point at which a changing stimulus causes a sensor to respond
  • Sensitivity - the degree to which a changing stimulus causes a sensor to change its response

Additionally, a sensor may respond to more than one stimulus in two ways: concurrently or sequentually. This means that sensors look for temporal relationships between stimulae.

  • Pattern - a responce to concurrent stimulae. This represents the concept of "space", the relationship between two or more stimulae at a single point in time.
  • Sequence - a response to temporal stimulae. This represents the concept of "time", the relationship between two or more stimulae at different points in time.
