Defining Intelligence

"The beginning of wisdom is the definition of terms" -Socrates.

Understanding intelligence is difficult because it involves ongoing, ever-evolving philosophical discussion and scientific research. Often ideas on the topic mean different things to different people. Definitions sometimes overlap with and involve other definitions, producing a confusing mess of terms. This is especially prevalent in discussions of consciousness, an extremely complex and involved topic. Furthermore the most plausible architecture of intelligence, the nervous system, is not yet well understood by the scientific community. Therefore, extracting salient principles of intelligence from biology and engineering seems beyond anyone's ability as of now. Despite the lack of consensus on the subject, I attept to define precise, distinct, and basic relavent terms to facilitate the discussions below.

  • Environment - where concepts and entities exist and have relation to each other
  • Stimulus - a detectable change in the environment
  • Sensor - that which responds to stimulus
  • Pattern - a responce to concurrent stimulae
  • Sequence - a response to temporal stimulae
  • Knowledge - patterns and sequences
  • Intelligence - the ability to aquire and apply knowledge
  • Sentience - the ability to experience sensations
  • Sapience - the ability to think about knowledge
  • Conscious - the ability to maintain an awareness of self and environment
