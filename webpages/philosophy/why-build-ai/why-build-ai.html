Human Cognitive History

All great works of man, the blessings of modernity, and the marvels of our species stem from the most important six inches of our lives, the brain. Since the dawn of our earliest ancestors, human cognitive labor has reaped a bountiful harvest. This crop of the mind includes an inexhaustible list of achievements and wonders: reason, science, engineering, philosophy, agriculture, the free market, democracy, art, music, literature, poetry, drama, fiction, and many other examples. Throughout history technological progress enabled men and women to do more with less. For example:

  • Agriculture & Medicine: longer lives and less suffering
  • The Loom: less-expensive and higher-quality clothing
  • The Printing Press: enabled the distribution of books causing educational revolution
  • The Internet: connects people across the globe
  • And many more examples...

By applying our intelligence strengthened by a powerful mammalian neocortex, our species survives and thrives in a world where a significant population of mankind has transcended a naturally vicious life of unnecessary suffering and propensity for animalistic violence. This does not overlook or excuse the unique malevolence spawned from human cognitive abilities or the troubles people face today or throughout history. In fact, constant vigilance of these concepts are crucially important for the survival of our species. However despite this dark side, the ultimate source of human success and prosperity thus far is due to our intelligence.

Despite the wonderful advancements, there are some natural limitations to human capabilities for productivity. An illustration of this can be found in the quote, "The richest place on Earth is the graveyard". Examples of our limitations

Human & AI Cognitive Future

As history has shown, because of our human limitations we build tools to survive and thrive.

Because of this profound success, the next logical (and perhaps inevitable) step is to build intelligent machines. It is important to note that the rate at which technology has grown so far is not linear, but exponential. The less capable yesterday's technology built the more capable today's technology which will build the even more capable tomorrow's technology. Thinking machines build better thinking machines to build better thinking machines....
